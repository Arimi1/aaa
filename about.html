Qorbuch vs Irikad, What’s the Difference?

There are a few differences between Qorbuch and Irikad, as they are seen mostly as dialects of each other, with Qorbuch being spoken in Tovuz as a minority, and Irikad spoken in Ganja as a minority. There’s also the city of Şəmkir which speaks a mixture of the two dialects. Qorbuch as some letters that Irikad does not, including some graves that Irikad doesn’t use (è, ù, ỳ, Irikad uses ò and à primarily). Those are diphthongs in Irikad and Qorbuch. È is the diphthong of “ew” or “eu”, ù is a diphthong of “uw”, ỳ is a diphthong of “yw” (only as a vowel), and both ò and à are diphthongs of “ou/ow” and “au/aw”. One exception of this rule would be sau or şau, the informal words for hello.

One major difference between Qorbuch and Irikad is that Qorbuch is more melodic than that of Irikad, which means Qorbuch has a speech melody more related to Norwegian and Swedish, than a bland and more boring speech melody of Irikad. Primarily, the two dialects just have minor spelling and pronunciation differences, but there are some words that need to be discussed that will trouble you on your journey to learn Irikad. Some word differences are:

Danrizhıyor - to understand (Qorbuch)

Sagarırum - to understand (Irikad)

Çevirırum - to translate (Qorbuch)
Severırum - to translate (Irikad) not much of a difference here, but in speech, there is a big pronunciation difference.

Maga - mother (Qorbuch)

Nənə - mother (Irikad)

There aren’t very many more major word differences, but it can be slightly  more difficult to understand for Irikad-only speakers. Such as these simple sentences for A tree didn’t do this:

Ber awla (àla) yog bom eçte. (Qorbuch)

Bir agla bem etüşte. (Irikad)

Another sentence, He is the one who admitted to killing him:

U nəfərem ù paltıyor awçte (àçte) um. (Qorbuch)

O nafarem, ò atşte om pəldırum. (Irikad)

As you can see, you will need to learn both forms in order to understand someone from Tovuz if you want to learn Irikad, but you can also still get by from just having a very open mind.

Infinitives in Qorbuch are also formed differently, they’re formed with -ıyor rather than -ırum like Irikad. Qorbuch also drops the Genitive case on the noun when forming the sentence My car, here’s an example:

Awtomobilmın (àtomobilmın) (Qorbuch)

Awtomobilıkmın (àtomobilıkmın) (Irikad)

The noun “awtomobil” in Irikad has the suffix -ık, which makes it Genitive, as in Qorbuch, the -ık is dropped, and it only adds a Genitive pronoun to the end.

About the Dialects

Qorbuçiye tili and Ganzhiye dili are the two standards (Qorbuch and Irikad), although the term Irikad also refers to Qorbuch and Irikad as a whole.

People don’t usually write in the transitional or local dialects, but they either learn to write Qorbuch or Irikad. However, in informal writing, transitional or local dialects seem to carry on. Qorbuçiye tili (Qorbuch dialect), also known as the Tovüziye tili (Tovuz dialect), is used for the Qorbuch standard, as Ganzhiye dili (Ganja dialect) is used for the Irikad standard. Although ir ykəd and ir dili are used in place of Ganzhiye dili most of the time.
